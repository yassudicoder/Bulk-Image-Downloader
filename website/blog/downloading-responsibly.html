Downloading images responsibly

The TheOpenBox team Feb 28, 2026 4 min read

Saving a few hundred images in one click is genuinely useful — for a mood board, a research archive, a design reference library. It's also easy to do thoughtlessly. A tool that makes bulk-downloading effortless is a good moment to be a little more deliberate, not less.

Here's the short checklist we'd give a friend.

1. "I can save it" isn't "I can use it"

Almost every image on the web is protected by copyright the instant it's created — no watermark or notice required. Being able to download a picture says nothing about what you're allowed to do with it. Saving a reference for private study is a very different thing from republishing it, printing it, or putting it in something you sell.

Before you reuse an image, check its licence. Look for a stated licence on the page, the site's terms, or metadata. When in doubt, assume "all rights reserved" and ask.

Downloading is a technical act. Using is a legal one. Keep the two separate in your head.

2. Prefer sources that want to be reused

If you need images you can actually use, start where the licensing is clear:

  • Public domain and CC0 collections (many museums, archives and stock libraries).
  • Creative Commons images — just mind the specific terms (attribution, non-commercial, no-derivatives).
  • Your own content, or images you've licensed directly.

Bulk Image Downloader's filters help here: narrow to one source domain, or to "only from this page", so you're pulling from a collection you've actually vetted rather than every third-party asset the page happened to load.

3. Read the room (and the terms)

Some sites explicitly prohibit bulk downloading or scraping in their terms of service. Others gate images behind an account precisely because they're licensed content. A tool can't know the rules of the site you're on — that part is on you. If a site says don't, don't.

4. Don't hammer the server

Every image you save is a request to someone else's server. Fetching hundreds of full-resolution files as fast as possible can look a lot like an attack, even when it isn't.

Bulk Image Downloader deliberately fetches with bounded concurrency — a handful of requests at a time, not a thousand at once — and reuses images the browser already loaded wherever it can. You can help too: filter down to what you actually want before you download, and resist re-scanning the same page over and over.

5. Attribute, and keep things tidy

If a licence asks for credit, give it. And future-you will appreciate a little organisation now: the folder rules feature can sort downloads into subfolders by source domain or date automatically, so a big grab doesn't become an undifferentiated heap in your Downloads folder.

None of this is complicated — it's mostly just remembering there's a person and a server on the other end. Use the tool to save yourself time, not to forget your manners.
